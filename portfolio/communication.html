Communication

Communication is integral to the work of engineers; this includes reading and writing reports, giving presentations, and liaising with teammates and/or clients (both orally and via written messages). I have developed strong communication skills over the past years from coursework, extra-curricular activities, and internships - I am able to communicate effectively via written medium like reports and emails in particular. However, I am developing skills to communicate my thoughts in a group setting, particularly when it comes to communicating my opinions in difficult situations involving negotiations or conflict resolution.

The entries below demonstrate how I have used my communication skills in the Design for the Environment course.

Client Communication

As the team leader, I am in charge of communicating with the client via email to set up client meetings and follow up with a meeting summary after each meeting. Specifically, my role includes the following tasks:

  • Creating and sending a meeting agenda
  • Liaising with team members to organize talking points during the meeting
  • Understanding the client's intention and taking notes during the meeting
  • Promptly sending a meeting summary after each meeting

Throughout the term, the client has been satistified with the team's self-initiative to reach out for questions and suggest ideas to make a better recommendation to the client - I believe this is partly because of how the team as a whole has stayed organized during client meetings to deliver information in a concise manner. By sending summaries after the meeting, I also help both the client and my team to ensure that both parties are on the same page about the progress of the project.

This experience allowed me to demonstrate and improve my communication skills, particularly in the context of hosting client meetings and communicating with clients. If I end up working in finance as per my career goal, I would be talking to multiple clients in a day to get new deals. As such, this aspect of the course directly ties into developing the skills necessary for my future career.

Visualizing Data Using Charts

For the term project in the Design for Environment course, my team was tasked with evaluating the environmental impacts of K-Cups and instant coffee, so that the client (Deloitte) can decide which option to use when providing coffee for employees. To communicate our recommendation, we submitted a 7500-word report outlining the environmental, economical, and societal impacts of both alternatives.

One of my role was to produce high-quality charts and graphics using Python and Excel to communicate the team's findings effectively. My particular area of focus was to visualize the environmental impacts in a way that enhanced the team's argument to recommend instant coffee over K-Cups. This experience taught me a few things:

  • I learned how to choose the best type of visualization based on the goal of the project.
  • I learned how to explore unorganized datasets to find meaningful information that could be visualized to strengthen the team's argument.
  • When coding visualization tools in Python, I learned best practices for commenting and writing my code so that other people can understand what I am trying to accomplish.

Overall, I have demonstrated my ability to communicate quantitative information in an effective manner using charts and other visualizations. This experience will directly tie to my upcoming PEY Co-op at Scotiabank, where I will be responsible for creating data dashboards to display insightful information so that senior stakeholders can make important business decisions. While the work I took on in this project is much smaller in scale than what I would work on as a co-op, a lot of the lessons learned can be carried over so that I can make a significant contribution on my team.
